History of Acrylics

History



Experimentation of acrylics began in the 1920s and 1930s when Mexican social realist muralist David Alfaro Siqueiros founded and held a workshop in New York City to experiment with upcoming paint mediums. By the late 1940s, acrylics were sold commercially by Leonard Bocour and Sam Golden under the name Magna, which were acrylics based in mineral water rather than water. It was designed mostly for industrial use.


Compared to other mediums, acrylic is a very modern medium. It has barely dug its roots in the past century. In the mid 19th century, German chemist Otto Röhm brought the potential of acrylic mediums to light. He secured a patent for acrylics as a paint binder, used mostly for industrial use.






In the 1950s, Röhm and his business partner Otto Haas introduced the first acrylic emulsion designed for painting. By 1955, the first commercially available water-based acrylic paint was placed on the market.
